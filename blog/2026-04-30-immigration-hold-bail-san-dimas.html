An immigration hold in San Dimas complicates an already stressful situation. Two completely separate legal systems run simultaneously — California criminal law and federal immigration law — and decisions in one affect the other. This guide explains what an ICE hold is, what it means for bail, and the critical steps to take before posting any bond.

This article is general information, not legal advice. ICE hold cases require both a criminal defense attorney and an immigration attorney. Do not make decisions based solely on this guide.

What an ICE Detainer Actually Is

An ICE detainer — officially Form I-247A — is a request from U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement to a local jail. It asks the jail to hold a person up to 48 hours past their criminal release time so ICE can take them into federal custody for removal proceedings.

Important: a detainer is a request, not a court-ordered warrant. Local jails decide whether to honor it. State and local laws can limit how often they actually do.

California's SB 54 (California Values Act)

California's Senate Bill 54, signed in 2017, restricts local cooperation with ICE. It prohibits state and local law enforcement from honoring ICE detainers except in specific circumstances:

  • Defendant has a serious or violent felony conviction within the past 15 years (PC §1192.7 or §667.5)
  • Current charge is a serious or violent felony
  • Specific aggravated felonies (sex crimes against minors, gang-related crimes)
  • Federal warrant signed by a judge (different from a detainer)

For most misdemeanors and many lower-level felonies, LASD will not hold the defendant past their criminal release time.

However: ICE can arrest someone separately at the courthouse, in the community, at home, or at work — even when SB 54 prevents jail-to-ICE transfer. Posting bail does not protect against community ICE enforcement.

Three Possible Outcomes When You Post Bail With an ICE Hold

  1. SB 54 protects release: The detainer is for an offense not covered by SB 54. The defendant is released to family per the criminal release process.
  2. SB 54 allows transfer: The detainer covers a serious felony or qualifying past conviction. The defendant transfers to ICE custody — typically to Adelanto ICE Processing Center.
  3. ICE arrest in the community: The defendant is released from the jail but ICE arrests them at the courthouse exit, on the way home, or later.

The bail premium (10% under California Insurance Code §1800.4) is non-refundable in all three scenarios. If the defendant ends up in ICE custody and misses a state court date, the bondsman may file for bond exoneration under PC §1305, but this requires legal process.

Critical Steps Before Posting Bail With an ICE Hold

  1. Confirm the ICE detainer. Call the booking facility (LASD San Dimas Station, (909) 450-2700) and ask whether an ICE detainer has been filed. Get the detainer number if possible.
  2. Hire a criminal defense attorney. They handle the underlying state charge.
  3. Hire an immigration attorney. Federal removal proceedings — separate specialty. Free legal aid:
    • Public Counsel Immigrant Rights Project: (213) 385-2977
    • Immigrant Defenders Law Center (ImmDef): (213) 634-0999
    • Coalition for Humane Immigrant Rights (CHIRLA): (213) 353-1333 (Spanish)
  4. Discuss strategy. Sometimes posting bail isn't the right move. If ICE will likely take custody anyway, fighting the criminal case from custody (where ICE can't easily transfer mid-case) may be better than posting and ending up in Adelanto.
  5. Understand the financial exposure. The 10% premium is non-refundable. Collateral pledged may be at risk if the defendant misses court due to ICE detention.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is an ICE detainer?
Form I-247A — a federal request asking the local jail to hold someone up to 48 hours past their criminal release time for ICE custody. A request, not a court warrant.
Can I bail someone out with an ICE hold?
Yes — bail can be posted on the criminal charge. But ICE may still take custody if SB 54 exceptions apply. Always consult both a criminal defense and immigration attorney first.
What happens if ICE takes them after I post bail?
10% premium non-refundable per CA Ins Code §1800.4. Defendant transfers to Adelanto or other federal facility. Missed state court dates may require bond exoneration filings under PC §1305.
Does San Dimas/LA County cooperate with ICE?
San Dimas transfers to LASD custody. Under SB 54, LASD honors detainers only for serious/violent felonies. Most misdemeanors are not held — but ICE can still arrest in the community.
What should families do first?
Hire BOTH criminal defense AND immigration attorney. Confirm detainer with booking facility. Discuss whether posting bail is strategically correct.
