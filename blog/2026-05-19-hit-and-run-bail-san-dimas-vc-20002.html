San Dimas-Specific Facts You Need

VC §20002 is one of the most under-explained statutes in California traffic law. The statute applies when a driver involved in an accident resulting only in damage to property (no injury, no death) fails to immediately stop and provide identification + insurance information to the other party. Violation is a misdemeanor with bail typically set at $1,500-$5,000 per the LA bail schedule for first-offense cases.

The distinction from VC §20001 matters enormously. VC §20001 is the felony hit-and-run statute — it applies when the accident resulted in injury or death. Bail for VC §20001 first-offense runs $50,000-$100,000 and carries 2-4 years state prison exposure. The DA can sometimes charge VC §20001 even in cases without obvious injury if any "soft-tissue" or "anxiety" injury is alleged — making early defense critical.

San Dimas hit-and-run cases are typically investigated by LASD San Dimas Station detectives over several days to weeks. The defendant often learns about the case via a phone call from a detective requesting a "voluntary statement" — which the defendant should decline without counsel present. Posting bail proactively (before arrest) isn't possible; the bond only attaches once a defendant is in custody. But retaining counsel before the warrant issues often results in a "walk-in" arraignment where the defendant appears voluntarily, signs an OR-release agreement, and avoids any custody time.

The civil/insurance interaction is the part most families miss. VC §20002 conviction (or even arrest) typically triggers an insurance non-renewal or rate spike for the driver. The DMV adds 2 points to the driving record under VC §12810. If three points accumulate within 12 months, license suspension follows. Defense strategy often focuses on civil compromise under PC §1377 — the alleged victim agrees the property damage is resolved, the defense files a compromise motion, and the criminal case dismisses. This requires paying the property damage (often a few thousand dollars) but avoids the misdemeanor conviction.

What VC §20002 Actually Says

California Vehicle Code §20002 covers: Misdemeanor hit-and-run involving property damage only — separate statute from VC §20001 (injury). Understanding the statute matters because the bail schedule, no-bail-hold rules, and protective-order rules all flow from how the prosecutor charges the case.

Three points families typically miss: the charging decision is not final at booking — the DA's filing review (within 48 hours under PC §825) can change the charge. Bail schedule amounts are not ceilings — judges can adjust based on flight risk or under In re Humphrey (2021) ability-to-pay. Protective orders attach automatically in many cases — PC §136.2 CPOs at arraignment may bar the defendant from returning home.

Bail Cost and the 10% Premium

The bail amount and the cost to post a bond are two different numbers. The bail amount is the dollar figure the court requires to guarantee appearance — set per the San Gabriel Valley bail schedule and subject to judicial discretion at arraignment.

The cost to a family is the 10% statutory premium under California Insurance Code §1800.4. This premium is a fee for the surety service, not money lent to the customer — it is non-refundable regardless of case outcome. The 10% rate is set by California Department of Insurance regulation; no licensed bondsman can charge a different rate. A bondsman quoting 5% or 8% is either operating outside the law or planning to add fees later.

Mistakes to Avoid in the First 24 Hours

  1. Don't wait for arraignment to act. Bail can be posted as soon as booking is complete.
  2. Don't pay cash bail without checking with a bondsman first. Cash bail ties up the full amount until case resolution.
  3. Don't sign indemnitor paperwork you haven't read. The indemnitor is on the hook for the full bail if the defendant fails to appear.
  4. Don't coach the defendant on what to say at booking. Phone calls from jail are recorded.
  5. Don't violate a protective order. Contact through any means under PC §136.2 can result in PC §166 charges.
